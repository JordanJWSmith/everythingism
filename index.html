Everythingism

Joe Hill

Recently:

HS2 is building a one kilometer-long tunnel in Buckinghamshire to prevent bats being harmed by high-speed trains, despite no evidence the trains would interfere with bats. The tunnel will cost over £100 million to build.
The Planning Inspectorate recommended against a new nuclear power station on the island of Anglesey in part because of concerns that new workers would dilute the number of Welsh speakers in the area.
Hospitals and schools will be told by the Government to buy more British fruit and vegetables to support the farming industry.
The Treasury is considering forcing pension funds to invest in UK assets in order to make capital cheaper for British firms.
The Town and Country Planning Act mandates that developers deliver "biodiversity net gain", an improvement in the overall quality of natural habitats by 10 per cent from any development.
The Department for Education's consultation on the new school curriculum asks consultees for views on how to shape the content of the curriculum to represent the diversity of British society.
Nutrient neutrality rules have blocked nearly 100,000 homes from being built across the country, despite agreement by all parties that the new homes would have a tiny impact on pollution in the area.
The Metropolitan Police Service has discontinued its Gangs Matrix database because of a legal challenge on the basis that it is unlawful for it to over-represent BAME men as potential intelligence leads.
The Teesside carbon capture project to offset CO2 in one of Britain's largest industrial areas commits industry to base at least 60 per cent of the supply chain in the UK, even if this incurs higher costs for taxpayers.
The Office for Nuclear Regulation almost forced EDF to install an additional safety vent in their reactor model despite it being unclear that there would be any safety benefit at all, at the cost of tens of millions of pounds. It is this approach, along with expensive environmental protections with minimal benefits like a 'fish disco', that means Hinkley Point C is set to be the most expensive nuclear power station of all time.

These examples may appear arbitrary, but they have a common root. They all exemplify a powerful force taking over the State: Everythingism.

Everythingism is why the Government can't have a policy that delivers safe and cheap nuclear power quickly. Britain has some of the highest energy prices in the world, and with increasing geopolitical instability energy security is a key government priority, yet moving to safe and cheap nuclear power is impossible unless it also promotes the Welsh language, enhances biodiversity, and upskills the local workforce.

Everythingism is the belief that every proposal, project or policy is a means for promoting every national objective, all at the same time. Trade policy is not for getting cheaper goods, but for reshaping the global economy. Planning policy is not for getting high-quality housing near the best jobs, but creating local jobs, hiring more apprentices, and enhancing biodiversity. And Government procurement isn't for the best products and services at the cheapest price. It's about promoting “social and economic value”, reshaping the British economy to promote Net Zero, regional economic development, and diversity and equality training.

Under Everythingism, we never do any one thing well, we do everything poorly. Housing policy is the main route for fixing the nitrogen imbalances that farmers create in local rivers, and creating more social housing to subsidise the welfare state. Trains must look after our bats. Climate policy is to support the services sector. Creating jobs, reducing pollution, boosting biodiversity are all laudable policy aims. So how can pushing them be bad? Because in each example of Everythingis, the policy is to deliver those goals through systems which are fundamentally designed with other objectives in mind. The goals aren't always at odds, but often they are. Sometimes that trade-off might be worth it, but often it isn't.

This isn't a new problem. When Governments have failed in the past, the spectre of Everythingism has been there. Concorde was set up to simultaneously be a joint Anglo- French industrial project, a demonstration of the most advanced aerospace engineering, and commercially viable. The result was a beautiful but financially unsustainable aircraft that only a handful of airlines could use, and required massive subsidies to keep it going. Britain isn't alone in Everythingism. In the US, Ezra Klein calls this “Everything-Bagel Liberalism”, the propensity of policymakers in blue states, especially in leading cities, to see any project as an opportunity to advance multiple goals. Affordable housing projects become unaffordable because of California's insistence on ideas about perfect development, which get in the way of plenty of ones which would be good enough. The security-critical semiconductor industry can't build new factories because of the National Environmental Policy Act, and Congress insisting on workforce diversity measures at semiconductor sites in the Inflation Reduction Act.

But Britain is a place where Everythingism is deeply rooted - and a profound problem for the country. It isn't just that it feels like everything is going wrong, or that everything needs to be fixed. The problem is that we're relying on everything to fix everything else, everywhere, all at once. And so nothing works.

Everything To Lose

The previous Conservative Government rejected plans for a new datacentre right next to the M25 on the grounds that it would “significantly alter the character and appearance of the area”. The land is currently a disused quarry, next to a major motorway, but its status as part of the Green Belt makes it easier to justify vetoing new developments. In Bradford, a development to create 140 new homes was delayed because the proposal did not adequately consider the speed of cricket balls. Britain's labyrinthine planning system builds in so many different requirements that it makes it easy for much needed developments to be blocked on the grounds that they disadvantage any one of a number of different goals.

There are 27 kinds of public bodies listed by government guidance as statutory consultees on different kinds of development, each with their own opportunity to add new requirements or request changes to a development. Internationally, we see far more homebuilding, and more affordable housing as a result, when jurisdictions allow simple processes which don't build in many complex requirements.

Everyone loses from Everythingism.

Everythingism is rarely explicit, but frequently implicit in public policy - a guiding sentiment that if policymakers don't use every tool at their disposal to further all objectives, they have failed to be imaginative enough. A sentiment which is very appealing to the vested interests who want to see their specific objectives furthered regardless of the costs to others, and who stand to gain the most from the government trying to 'do it all'.

Everythingism is less common in the world of business; it’s rarely tolerated for long by competitive markets. Successful teams tend to divide labour and specialise. Successful companies are rewarded for focus – it’s impossible to imagine Steve Jobs or Mark Zuckerberg starting successful companies by trying a hundred different interconnected projects, at the same time, and doing them all badly. Globally, countries succeed based on the same principles, specialising in providing the goods and services they are best placed to trade.

Many theories underpin the more focused approach. But all of them are supported by basic common sense: you can’t do it all, some ways of doing things are better than others, and there are nearly always trade-offs.

Governments try to advance their objectives through many different complex instruments - policies, legislation, economic measures, national infrastructure and public services. Not only do they struggle to prioritise between objectives, but also to identify which instruments are best used to deliver those objectives.

Policymakers shouldn't ignore complexity. After all, many policy instruments do affect many different outcomes, and pretending they don't would be misleading. The world is more interconnected than ever; by global markets, the banking system, the internet, and unprecedented levels of global migration. The sudden and far-reaching impact of pandemics, wars, technological developments and economic crises is proof that organisations, systems and countries are more intertwined than ever, and that the system requires deep systems-thinking to improve.

But while complexity is helpful as a description of the situation, it is unhelpful as a prescription for action. It might be tempting to believe we can optimise every policy system for every policy objective, all at once, but that is fantasy. It leads to distraction, confusion about the policy objectives being delivered, and paralysis. Ruthless prioritisation can be uncomfortable, but delivering any meaningful change through Everythingism is unrealistic.

In the past, this kind of focus has rewarded British policymakers who have rejected Everythingism. Some have succeeded by ploughing through objections and vested interests, and setting clear focus and objectives, particularly in times of national crisis. The Vaccine Taskforce depended on officials working outside of the normal processes for procurement, hiring and public spending. Academisation in education, which focused on freeing up schools to specialise, rather than getting them to do everything everywhere, has been a multi-decade success.

The Contemporary Resurgence

Modern developments across numerous fields have breathed new life into these ancient intuitions. Quantum physics has revealed the strange entanglement of particles across vast distances. Ecological sciences have mapped the intricate web of relationships that sustain planetary life. Information theory has provided frameworks for understanding how patterns propagate across seemingly unrelated domains.

These scientific advances have converged with technological developments that make interconnection not just a theoretical concept but a lived reality. The internet, social media, global transportation, and instantaneous communication have created a world where the butterfly effect is no longer merely metaphorical — the actions of individuals can and do ripple across the global system with unprecedented speed and impact.

Implications for Knowledge

If everything is indeed connected to everything else, then our traditional approach to knowledge — dividing reality into discrete disciplines and domains — may be fundamentally limited. The boundaries between physics and biology, between economics and psychology, between art and mathematics begin to appear more as artifacts of our cognitive limitations than as reflections of reality's true structure.

This doesn't mean that specialization has no value. Rather, it suggests that specialists must remain aware of the artificial nature of their boundaries, and develop methods for communicating across these boundaries. Transdisciplinary approaches, meta-frameworks, and comprehensive thinking styles become not just intellectually fashionable but practically necessary.

"The true expert is not one who knows everything about something, but one who understands how their something relates to everything."

Living Everythingism

Beyond its theoretical implications, everythingism offers a practical philosophy for navigating an increasingly complex world. It encourages us to be mindful of the extended consequences of our actions, to seek out unexpected connections between problems and solutions, and to maintain intellectual humility in the face of systems whose full complexity exceeds our comprehension.

This philosophy manifests in practical approaches like systems thinking, in cultural movements emphasizing global consciousness, and in technological innovations designed to make connections visible and tangible. It informs emergent forms of governance that acknowledge our interdependence, economic models that account for externalities, and educational approaches that emphasize contextual understanding alongside specialized knowledge.

Challenges and Criticisms

Of course, everythingism is not without its critics. Some argue that it threatens to dissolve important distinctions, leading to a kind of intellectual and moral relativism. Others worry that the emphasis on interconnection can paralyze action by making every decision seem overwhelmingly complex. Still others suggest that everythingism is simply too vague a concept to provide practical guidance in specific situations.

These criticisms merit serious consideration. An everythingist perspective need not deny the utility of boundaries and categories in particular contexts, nor should it prevent decisive action when required. The key lies in maintaining awareness of these boundaries as tools rather than truths — useful for certain purposes, but always provisional and partial.

Conclusion: Toward an Everything Future

As we move deeper into the 21st century, the relevance of everythingism seems likely to grow rather than diminish. The challenges we face — from climate change to artificial intelligence, from pandemic response to economic inequality — all resist siloed approaches and demand integrated understanding.

Perhaps the ultimate promise of everythingism lies not in providing final answers, but in fostering a particular quality of attention — one that remains open to the vast web of relationships that constitute our world, that seeks connections across traditional boundaries, and that approaches complexity not as an obstacle to understanding but as the very substance of the reality we seek to understand.

In embracing everythingism, we may discover that the truth of existence lies not in any particular thing, but in the infinite ways that all things relate to one another — a dynamic, ever-evolving tapestry that we both observe and help to weave.
